# Advisory Verification: A Deterministic Symbolic Step-Check Over-Rejects Correct Physics Reasoning in Tree-of-Thought Search

## Abstract

Tree-of-Thought search pairs a language model with a step-verifier that prunes branches it deems incorrect. We show that a deterministic SymPy "hard-rule" boundary-condition *grounding* check systematically over-rejects correct physics reasoning: it vetoes a node whenever a boundary-condition key fails to lexically match that node's own equation symbols (e.g., `v_initial` vs. `v0`, or descriptive keys like "level ground implies y=0"), producing false-negative prunes of valid derivations. Our fix demotes this check to *advisory*: the violation is logged but never fatal, so a branch is never deleted on a mere name mismatch. In a paired A/B on 15 held-out multi-step physics problems (identical configuration, only the gate flipped), advisory grounding raises mean solve rate by **+24.4 percentage points** for Qwen3.5-9B (0.689 → 0.933, 3 reps) and **+28.9 points** for Qwen3.6-35B-a3b (0.711 → 1.000, 3 reps), with zero correct-answer prunes under advisory versus several under hard gating. We distill a generalizable design rule: a deterministic step-check may safely *hard-gate* only if its predicate is **identity-independent** — decidable from a node's own symbols without resolving label-to-symbol identity; identity-dependent checks must remain advisory. We report these results plainly as a small study: two Qwen-family models on a single local backend, with the grounding check's measured precision on this corpus effectively zero (no true positives observed on naturally occurring boundary conditions).

## 1 Introduction

Tree-of-Thought (ToT) search [yao2023tot] and its relatives — MCTS-style planning over world models [hao2023rap], language-agent tree search with learned value functions [zhou2024lats], and graph-structured reasoning [besta2024got] — have become a dominant recipe for getting language models to solve problems that demand long, precise chains of intermediate steps. Multi-step college physics is a canonical stress test: even strong models struggle on it [scibench; physreason; phybench], and a single arithmetic slip, sign error, or misapplied theorem propagates to a wrong final answer. The natural response is to verify reasoning at the step level rather than only at the end. Process reward models [lightman2023verify; wang2024mathshepherd] and step-level physics scorers [physreason] judge each step in isolation; neuro-symbolic checkers formalize each step into logic or hand it to a solver for a deterministic verdict [VeriCoT; Logic-LM]. A deterministic symbolic step-verifier is especially appealing inside a tree search: it never hallucinates a pass, it is cheap, and it promises to prune wrong branches before they consume the search budget.

We built exactly such a system — an external ToT engine with a per-node finite-state machine (propose, calculate, evaluate, reflect, finalize), a frontier scheduler with pruning and depth bounds, role-split model routing, and a deterministic SymPy "hard-rule" step-verifier — and ran it over a development corpus of 90 multi-step physics problems plus 15 held-out problems spanning mechanics, EM, quantum, thermodynamics, optics, relativity, fluids, rotation, and simple harmonic motion. The verifier did the opposite of what we expected. One of its checks — a boundary-condition *grounding* rule — vetoes a node whenever a boundary-condition key is not a literal symbol-token of that node's own equations. But correct derivations routinely name the same physical quantity two ways (`v_initial` vs. `v0`, `T = "20 C"`, descriptive keys like `"level ground implies y=0"`), so the check fires on perfectly valid work. A constructed demonstrator node that derives `d = 7.2 m` is deleted under hard gating purely because its boundary key does not lexically match its equation symbols. The verifier built to protect the search was, in effect, deleting correct reasoning — the same over-rejection failure mode reported for process reward models [song2025prmbench; REVEAL], rule-based verifiers in RL [TinyV], and verifier-guided search [ScalingFlaws; fetch]. This is also a regime where simply adding more inference-time machinery can hurt rather than help [more-llm-calls].

Our fix is small and conservative: demote the offending check from fatal to advisory. The violation is still logged, but it can never delete a branch on a name mismatch; because low scores never prune, the search still explores. We then ask the deeper question this bug exposes — *when is a deterministic step-check actually safe to use as a hard gate?* — and answer it with a precision analysis and a generalizable design criterion.

Our contributions are:

- **An advisory-verification fix.** We make boundary-condition grounding advisory rather than fatal in every context (env-gated, default on). In a paired A/B (Section 4), advisory grounding lifts mean solve rate by +24.4 pp on Qwen3.5-9B and +28.9 pp on Qwen3.6-35B-a3b, driving correct-answer prunes to zero on both models.
- **A cross-model A/B.** The effect holds across two capability tiers of the Qwen family — a 9B dense model and a 35B MoE (~3B active) — on one backend, and slightly strengthens on the larger model. This is consistent with the over-rejection being a property of the verifier rather than of a single model's weakness, within the scope we tested.
- **A grounding-precision analysis.** Instrumented over 57 real problem contexts, the grounding check fired 59 times and caught **zero** genuine errors; the only inputs it correctly flags are hand-built adversarial garbage. A prototype "smart" predicate removes 97% of fires while retaining 4/4 adversarial detection, so a discriminating gate is feasible but, on this corpus, unnecessary.
- **An identity-(in)dependence gateability criterion.** A deterministic step-check may safely hard-gate iff its predicate is identity-*independent* — decidable from the node's own symbols without resolving label-to-symbol identity. Identity-*dependent* checks (like boundary grounding) must be advisory.
- **A measurement-artifact methodological note.** An early "1 / 15 solved" reading was a wall-clock artifact of an aggressive time cap; given adequate time the system solves ~93–100% — a cautionary example of how a measurement harness can report the very failure it appears to detect.

## 2 Related Work

**Structured search over LLM reasoning.** Tree-of-Thought search organizes generation as a tree of partial solutions, expanding and pruning branches under an LLM self-evaluation signal [yao2023tot]. Subsequent work casts this search as reward-guided planning: RAP frames reasoning as MCTS over a world model with probabilistic reward [hao2023rap], LATS unifies reasoning, acting, and planning using LM value functions and self-reflection as soft scores [zhou2024lats], and Graph-of-Thoughts generalizes the topology to arbitrary graphs with thought aggregation and feedback [besta2024got]. A recurring theme is that *soft*, value-weighted evaluation lets the search retain promising-but-unvalidated steps rather than deleting them. Our work targets the opposite regime: a *hard* deterministic gate that fatally prunes branches, and we show that demoting it to advisory (low scores never prune) recovers correct derivations the strict rejecter discards.

**Process-reward and step-level verifiers.** Process reward models (PRMs) score individual reasoning steps to judge correctness [lightman2023verify], and Math-Shepherd builds such per-step verifiers without human annotation, using them to rerank candidate solutions [wang2024mathshepherd]. These define the step-verification paradigm whose over-rejection cost we measure. PRMBench shows that PRMs both miss real errors and incorrectly flag valid steps [song2025prmbench], and FETCH attributes under-exploration in tree search to high-variance, noisy verifier scoring [fetch] — the search-efficiency analogue of our finding that a brittle step-check prunes correct branches. These verifiers are *learned* and probabilistic; ours is a *deterministic* symbolic predicate, so its failures are systematic rather than stochastic.

**Self-verification limits.** A separate line shows that LLM self-verification is itself unreliable: LLMs cannot reliably self-correct reasoning without external feedback, and intrinsic self-correction can degrade performance [huang2023selfcorrect]; self-critique collapses while *sound external verification* yields the gains [stechly2024selfverification]. This motivates an external, deterministic check — but it does not guarantee that the check is *trustworthy enough to gate*. Our results are an instance of the converse failure mode: a sound-looking external symbolic check that is itself a false-negative generator.

**Neuro-symbolic step validation.** Neuro-symbolic methods formalize LLM steps for symbolic checking: VeriCoT translates each chain-of-thought step into first-order logic and tests logical validity [VeriCoT]; Logic-LM runs deterministic symbolic solvers over LLM-translated formalizations [Logic-LM]; and REVEAL benchmarks verifiers of reasoning chains, finding step-level verifiers unreliable especially at judging logical correctness [REVEAL]. These are the closest precedents for our deterministic SymPy step-verifier. Logic-LM in particular exposes the brittleness we exploit: when the LLM's formalization is imperfect — here, when boundary-condition keys do not lexically match equation symbols — a deterministic check over that formalization rejects correct content. Prior work proposes such verifiers; we quantify the failure when one of their predicates is identity-dependent and supply a criterion for when symbolic checks may be trusted to gate at all.

**Verifier false-negatives and verifier-guided search.** The work nearest to ours studies verifier false-negatives. TinyV documents that rule-based verifiers wrongly reject correct outputs (>38% false negatives) and that recovering them improves the reward signal and pass rate [TinyV], and Scaling Flaws shows imperfect verifiers misrank candidates and can prune *all* valid paths in mathematical reasoning [ScalingFlaws]. More broadly, scaling test-time compute via votes-and-filters is non-monotonic and can hurt accuracy when the filter over-rejects [more-llm-calls]. The delta from this prior work is explicit: those false-negative analyses concern *learned* verifiers (PRMs) or *final-answer* equivalence checking (string/rule matching of outputs). Ours is a *deterministic symbolic check on intermediate physics steps*, where the false negative arises not from answer-string mismatch but from comparing differently-named tokens for the same physical quantity inside a derivation. Beyond replicating the phenomenon, we contribute a **gateability criterion** (Section 3.7) — a distinction prior work does not draw.

**Physics benchmarks.** Scientific and physics reasoning benchmarks establish both the difficulty and the need for step-level judgment. SciBench shows top LLMs solve only ~43% of college-level science problems [scibench]; PhysReason introduces step-level physics scoring that isolates theorem-application and process bottlenecks [physreason]; and PHYBench supplies 500 original physics problems with expression-level evaluation [phybench]. These motivate verifier-assisted physics pipelines and provide the kind of multi-step corpus on which over-strict symbolic rejection is measurable. Our evaluation is complementary and smaller in scope, and we are candid (Section 5) that the grounding check's observed precision on our corpus is ~0.

## 3 System and Method

### 3.1 Overview

We study an external Tree-of-Thought reasoning system for multi-step physics, in the structured-search-over-LLM lineage of ToT [yao2023tot], its graph generalization [besta2024got], and search-as-planning frameworks [hao2023rap; zhou2024lats]. The system has four components: a per-node finite-state machine (FSM), a tree scheduler, role-split model routing, and a deterministic SymPy "hard-rule" step-verifier. Our central contribution is a precise account of *when* such a verifier may safely prune a branch, and a fix that demotes one class of check from fatal to advisory.

### 3.2 Node finite-state machine

Each node is built by an FSM with five states, `PROPOSE → CALCULATE → EVALUATE → REFLECT → FINALIZED`. `PROPOSE` emits one minimal next-step candidate (a thought step, equations, known variables, used models, and boundary conditions); `CALCULATE` runs the deterministic hard-rule check over those equations and variables; `EVALUATE` applies a fixed weighted rubric (domain consistency 50%, variable grounding 25%, contextual relevance 10%, simplicity 15%); `REFLECT` revises a draft on a recoverable issue. Crucially, low rubric scores never delete a node — they only reduce downstream expansion priority. Only a hard-rule *domain* violation can move a node to `PRUNED_BY_RULE`; missing-context and other recoverable deterministic issues are routed back through `REFLECT`, and root nodes are special-cased so one bad first draft cannot collapse a session. This is the soft, value-weighted exploration posture argued for in MCTS- and PRM-style work [zhou2024lats; hao2023rap; lightman2023verify].

### 3.3 Tree scheduler

A scheduler reuses the node FSM and adds tree-level behavior: a depth-biased frontier, sibling ranking, expansion-budget and tree-depth bounds, frontier-size and per-diversity-key caps, state-signature deduplication, loop suppression over ancestor signatures, and duplicate-branch merge. Expanded children are ranked, the expandable ones placed on the frontier under a candidate budget, and the frontier rebalanced. The scheduler is the only component that creates or deletes nodes, so a verifier "veto" takes effect by setting a node's status, which the scheduler then treats as pruned.

### 3.4 Role-split model routing

Reasoning is split across model roles — planning (coarse, route-splitting session plan), modeling (one atomic next-step candidate), review (rubric scoring and any model-proposed hard-rule violations), and lightweight non-terminal evaluation — each dispatched to a configured local model served via LM Studio. Defaults route all roles to a single model; our A/B compares Qwen3.5-9B (dense) against Qwen3.6-35B-a3b (MoE, ~3B active; configured as `qwen3.6-35b-a3b-ud-mlx`). This routing is orthogonal to gating: the verifier's authority over a branch is identical regardless of which model produced the candidate.

### 3.5 Deterministic SymPy hard-rule verifier

The hard-rule check is a single deterministic skill invoked at `CALCULATE` (and over review-supplied violations at `EVALUATE`). It runs a battery of decidable predicates over the node's own symbols: dimensional equalities (via SymPy dimension maps), positivity/nonzero/finiteness of named variables (via SymPy assumptions), required/forbidden equation, model, and boundary-condition patterns, and a set of *semantic boundary-condition* checks. Violations are partitioned into (i) recoverable issues that trigger reflection and (ii) domain violations that prune. The verifier is the authoritative source of rule-based veto decisions; it is exactly the deterministic symbolic step-checker whose over-rejection the neuro-symbolic and PRM literature flags as a failure mode [VeriCoT; Logic-LM; lightman2023verify; wang2024mathshepherd; song2025prmbench; REVEAL].

### 3.6 The boundary-grounding check, defined precisely

Among the semantic boundary checks, the *boundary-grounding* check vetoes a node when a boundary-condition key (or, in the `axis = value` form, the constrained axis name) is not a textual symbol-token of that node's own equation symbols or known-variable names. Formally, let `S` be the set of symbol tokens appearing in the node's equations together with the names of its known variables. For a boundary-condition entry the check first strips a descriptive label prefix (e.g., `level ground implies y=0`); for a bare key it fires when the key's symbol tokens are non-empty and *none* lie in `S`, and for an `axis = value` key it fires when the (non-descriptive) `axis` token is not in `S`. The grounding predicate is therefore a comparison between *differently named tokens* — the BC label versus the equation symbol — for what is often the same physical quantity.

This is the bug. The modeling model routinely writes BC keys that do not lexically match its compressed equation symbols: `v_initial` vs. `v0`, `omega_final` vs. `omega`, unit-bearing numeric constraints such as `T = "20 C"`, and purely descriptive keys. Each such legitimate boundary condition makes grounding fire, and because grounding was a *domain* violation, the node was moved to `PRUNED_BY_RULE`. A constructed demonstrator (`experiments/demo_delete_vs_advise.py`) reproduces this deterministically: a node deriving `d = 7.2 m` with a BC keyed `v_f = 0` is deleted under hard gating and retained under advisory, purely on the name mismatch. Instrumented over 57 real problem contexts, the check fired 59 times, of which 56 (95%) were on descriptive (non-numeric-valued) BCs, and it caught **zero** genuine errors — the same over-rejection pathology reported for rule-based verifiers elsewhere [TinyV; ScalingFlaws; song2025prmbench; stechly2024selfverification].

### 3.7 Advisory vs. hard gating, and the gateability criterion

We define a check's authority as one of two modes. A **hard** (fatal) check, on firing, contributes a domain violation that prunes the node to `PRUNED_BY_RULE`; no soft score can rescue it. An **advisory** check still computes and emits its violation for observability, but the violation is routed to an `ignored_violations` log and is never fatal in any context. Because low rubric scores already never prune, an advisory verdict leaves the branch fully explorable; the verifier loses only its ability to *delete* a branch on that signal, not its ability to *report* it.

**The fix** makes boundary-grounding advisory in every context. The grounding violation (both bare-key and `axis = value` forms) is emitted but filtered into `ignored_violations` before the prune/recover partition, so a correct derivation can no longer be deleted on a name mismatch. The behavior is gated by the environment variable `TOT_BOUNDARY_GROUNDING_FIX` (default `1` = advisory; `0` = hard gating). Critically, advisory is scoped to grounding *only*: the co-located *intrinsic* boundary check — "a boundary value depends on the constrained axis," e.g., a Dirichlet value `u(x,t)` on `x = 0` — remains a fatal violation, because it is decidable from the node's own symbols without any cross-naming.

The fix instantiates a general design rule. We say a deterministic step-check is **identity-independent** iff its predicate is decidable from the node's own symbols *without resolving any label↔symbol identity* — without asserting that two differently-named tokens denote the same physical quantity. We adopt the criterion:

> **Gateability criterion.** A deterministic step-check may safely HARD-GATE (fatally prune) iff it is identity-*independent*. Identity-*dependent* checks must be advisory.

The intrinsic checks the system keeps fatal — dimensional consistency, positivity/nonzero/finiteness, and "value depends on the constrained axis" — are identity-independent: each is decided purely over the node's own symbols and SymPy structure. Boundary-*grounding* is identity-dependent: it can only be satisfied by recognizing that `v_initial` and `v0` are the same quantity, an identity-resolution step a deterministic token-matcher cannot soundly perform. Under the criterion it must be advisory, and the system demotes exactly this check while leaving the identity-independent battery fatal.

## 4 Experiments and Results

### 4.1 Setup

We evaluate a single design change — demoting boundary-condition grounding from a fatal prune to an advisory log — while holding everything else fixed. The only manipulated variable is the gate flag (`TOT_BOUNDARY_GROUNDING_FIX`): the *advisory* condition logs grounding violations but never deletes a branch; the *hard* condition (`=0`) restores the original fatal pruning. The intrinsic "value depends on the constrained axis" check remains fatal in both conditions; only the cross-naming grounding check is flipped.

Three problem sets are involved and kept distinct: a **development corpus** of 90 multi-step physics problems; a **held-out A/B set** of 15 fresh problems spanning mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum (the infinite square well), thermodynamics, optics, relativity, fluids, rotation, and SHM, disjoint from the development corpus; and **57 instrumented BC-bearing problem contexts** used for the grounding-precision audit (Section 4.3), each context being one captured `(equations, known_vars, boundary_conditions)` instance on which the grounding check ran.

We run two locally served Qwen-family models: Qwen3.5-9B (dense) and Qwen3.6-35B-a3b (MoE, ~3B active), both via LM Studio. For each model and condition we run **three paired repetitions per model** under identical configuration (three runs per condition for each model). Execution is local and serial; the concurrency path is unusable on this backend (Section 5). The primary metric is *solve rate*: the fraction of the 15 held-out problems answered correctly. We additionally count *correct-answer prunes* (nodes deriving the correct answer that were nonetheless deleted).

For result integrity we note two engineering corrections made before these runs. First, a *fabricated-passing-score* bug in an off-by-default local-fallback evaluation mode was found and fixed; the A/B was run with that mode disabled, so the reported solve rates are not affected by it. Second, five unrelated correctness bugs were fixed and the system carries 178 passing automated tests covering the verifier and harness.

### 4.2 A/B results

Making grounding advisory raises solve rate on both models.

**Qwen3.5-9B (dense), 3 reps**

| Condition | Rep 1 | Rep 2 | Rep 3 | Mean | Correct-answer prunes |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|------|-----------------------|
| Advisory  | 0.933 | 1.000 | 0.867 | 0.933 | 0 / 0 / 0 |
| Hard      | 0.667 | 0.667 | 0.733 | 0.689 | 5 / 5 / 3 |

Delta = **+24.4 pp** (0.933 vs. 0.689). All three advisory reps exceed all three hard reps.

**Qwen3.6-35B-a3b (MoE, ~3B active), 3 reps**

| Condition | Rep 1 | Rep 2 | Rep 3 | Mean | Correct-answer prunes |
|-----------|-------|-------|-------|------|-----------------------|
| Advisory  | 1.000 | 1.000 | 1.000 | 1.000 | 0 / 0 / 0 |
| Hard      | 0.733 | 0.733 | 0.667 | 0.711 | 3 / 3 / 4 |

Delta = **+28.9 pp** (1.000 vs. 0.711). *Note:* all three advisory reps sit at the 100% ceiling, so the advisory "range" is a single point; the non-overlap with the hard condition is therefore bounded by the ceiling rather than by observed variance (see the ceiling limitation in Section 5).

The effect holds across both capability tiers and is slightly larger on the 35B (+28.9 vs. +24.4 pp). Under advisory grounding, zero correct-answer prunes occur on either model; under hard gating, correct derivations are destroyed in every repetition (5, 5, and 3 on the 9B; 3, 3, 4 on the 35B). This is the over-rejection failure mode that prior work documents for verifier-guided search and rule-based verifiers [TinyV; ScalingFlaws; song2025prmbench], here arising from a deterministic symbolic step-check inside a Tree-of-Thought search [yao2023tot]. With three single-seed reps per model we report observed values and ranges, not confidence intervals or significance tests; the evidential weight is the consistent direction and the separation of observed ranges on this 15-problem set (Section 5).

### 4.3 Grounding-precision analysis

To characterize *why* the grounding check over-rejects, we instrumented it over 57 real problem contexts and observed 59 fires.

| Quantity | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Problem contexts instrumented | 57 |
| Grounding fires observed | 59 |
| Fires on descriptive (non-numeric-valued) BCs | 56 / 59 (95%) |
| Fires eliminated by a "smart" predicate prototype | 57 / 59 (97%) |
| Adversarial-garbage cases still detected (smart predicate) | 4 / 4 |
| Genuine errors caught (any fire) | 0 (no true positives) |

Fires are dominated by descriptive, non-numeric BCs (e.g., `"level ground implies y=0"`, `"isothermal = T=300K const"`) and by cross-naming mismatches (`v_initial` vs. `v0`, `omega_final` vs. `omega`, `T = "20 C"`). A prototype "smart" predicate — exempting non-numeric BCs and normalizing names — removes 57 of 59 fires while still flagging all 4 of 4 hand-built adversarial-garbage inputs. The two residual numeric flags (`x(0)=0`, `v_initial=0`) are themselves legitimate boundary conditions the node expressed with a different symbol — i.e., they too are false negatives. **Across all 59 fires the check produced zero true positives**; the only inputs it correctly flags are hand-constructed adversarial BCs, so its precision on naturally occurring boundary conditions in this corpus is 0. The 4/4 adversarial retention shows a discriminating gate is *feasible*, not that the existing check has real-world discrimination. This is consistent with reports that step-level verifiers both miss errors and wrongly flag valid steps [song2025prmbench; REVEAL; stechly2024selfverification].

### 4.4 Measurement-artifact correction

An early reading suggested the system "rarely completes" — only "1 / 15 solved." We trace this to a wall-clock measurement artifact: cases were capped at 140–240 s, while the 9B needs roughly 250–480 s per case under our setup. Given adequate time the same set solves at 14 / 15 (0.933). We therefore retract the "rarely completes" claim; the apparent near-total failure was an instrumentation timeout, not a reasoning failure — an illustration of how time-capped harnesses can report false negatives of their own.

## 5 Discussion and Limitations

**Why advisory wins here.** The empirical case for advisory verification is strong and consistent on this corpus, and it rests on one decisive fact: the boundary-grounding check has essentially no discriminating value here. Over 57 real contexts it fired 59 times and caught zero genuine errors — every real fire was a false negative, and 95% were on descriptive BCs. When a gate's observed precision is ~0, removing its authority can only help. Flipping the gate from fatal to advisory raised mean solve rate by +24.4 pp on the 9B and +28.9 pp on the 35B, and drove correct-answer prunes to zero on both models. The effect generalizes across the two tiers and slightly strengthens on the larger one.

A subtler point follows. One might reach for a "smart" gate that keeps fatal pruning but avoids these mistakes — and such a gate is feasible: our prototype eliminates 97% of fires while retaining 4/4 adversarial detection. But on this corpus it is unnecessary. Since the check never caught a real error here, advisory logging captures all of the value a smart gate would and none of its residual false-negative risk. Engineering a discriminating gate is the right move only when the check has demonstrable precision to preserve; here it does not — and the only positive evidence for any gate is against synthetic garbage, not naturally occurring errors.

**The general lesson: gateability.** The transferable rule is the gateability criterion of Section 3.7: a deterministic step-check may safely hard-gate iff its predicate is identity-*independent*. Identity-*dependent* checks, which compare differently-named tokens for the same physical quantity, must be advisory, because a name mismatch is not evidence of an incorrect derivation. Our system keeps the identity-independent "value depends on the constrained axis" check fatal while demoting the cross-naming grounding check to advisory. This separates the cases where a symbolic verifier genuinely adds safety from those where it merely punishes naming variation — a distinction relevant to structured-search methods whose self-evaluation prunes branches [yao2023tot; besta2024got] and to step-level verifiers more broadly, which are known both to miss errors and to flag valid steps [song2025prmbench; REVEAL; TinyV; ScalingFlaws].

**Limitations.** We state the caveats plainly; the result is real but its scope is narrow.

- **Small held-out set and few reps.** The A/B uses n = 15 held-out problems (alongside the 90-problem development corpus), with **three single-seed reps per model**. We report observed ranges, not confidence intervals or significance tests; each condition rests on only three runs per model. No formal significance is claimed.
- **Narrow model and backend coverage.** We test two Qwen-family models on a single local backend (LM Studio). "Capability-robust" here means only that the effect holds across these two tiers; we make no claim about other families, scales, or serving stacks.
- **Ceiling on the larger model.** Advisory reaches 100% on all three 35B reps, leaving no headroom to separate advisory from a hypothetical better gate or to quantify how much further the method could go.
- **Grounding precision is ~0 on this corpus.** Our claim that a smart discriminating gate is unnecessary is conditional on the observed precision being ~0 here (no true positives on real BCs; the only positives are adversarial/hand-built). On corpora where grounding would catch genuine errors, the trade-off changes and a discriminating gate — which we show is feasible — could be warranted.
- **Concurrency path unusable on this backend.** LM Studio splits its context window across parallel slots and ToT's large prompts overflow, so all results are sequential and we cannot report throughput.
- **Measurement caution.** An early "1 / 15 solved" reading was a wall-clock artifact (cases capped at 140–240 s while the 9B needs ~250–480 s/case); given adequate time the system solves ~93–100%. We report this as a retraction of the "rarely completes" claim and a reminder that solve-rate measurements under time caps can be badly misleading.

## 6 Conclusion

We showed that a deterministic SymPy step-verifier embedded in a Tree-of-Thought reasoning system [yao2023tot] systematically over-rejects correct physics derivations: its identity-dependent boundary-condition grounding check fired 59 times across 57 real contexts, caught zero genuine errors, and produced false-negative prunes of correct nodes. Demoting that check from a hard gate to an advisory signal lifted mean held-out solve rate by +24.4 pp on Qwen3.5-9B (0.689 → 0.933) and +28.9 pp on Qwen3.6-35B-a3b (0.711 → 1.000), with correct-answer prunes falling to zero on both models — an effect that holds across both tiers and slightly strengthens on the larger one. The broader takeaway is a concrete design rule: because LLM-driven and symbolic verifiers are known to wrongly reject valid reasoning [stechly2024selfverification; song2025prmbench; TinyV; ScalingFlaws], unreliable step-checks should be treated as advisory by default, and hard gating should be reserved for identity-independent predicates decidable from a node's own symbols without resolving label-to-symbol identity. These findings rest on a modest evaluation — 15 held-out problems atop a 90-problem corpus, two Qwen-family models on a single local backend, few reps without significance testing, and an advisory ceiling of 1.000 on the 35B set — which future work should extend with stronger models, larger and harder physics corpora [phybench; physreason], and lineage-aware grounding predicates that normalize cross-naming between labels and equation symbols.
